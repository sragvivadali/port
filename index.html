Paper Rockets Design

Objective: The objective was to design, create, test, and conclude on a specific projectile using paper and tape to travel the longest distance.


In this project, my team and I needed to create a projectile that can travel a large distance. During this project, my main contribution was to create two designs of projectiles. For this project, we needed to create a projectile that can travel the farthest. This distance was tracked vertically. For my projectile, I decided to create a rocket. This rocket was created using printer paper and transparent packaging tape. When we were testing the rockets, I decided that 45 degrees was the most efficient angle to launch the rocket and it traveled 31 yards in 10 seconds at 60psi. This was due to the wind catching the rocket and caused it to slow down faster. Seeing my mistakes, I created another rocket similar to the previous rocket. I created the body using two papers, cardstock and printer paper, decided to decrease the fins size so that it doesn't catch onto the wind. I also wrapped the entire rocket with duct tape and increase the compression of the rocket. This rocket traveled 88 yards in approximately 3 seconds. Another aspect of this project is creating a 2d simulation using NetLogo. Using Aerodynamic equations, I was able to create a simulation for a rocket launch adding factors such as wind and initial velocity. This helped me find the best angle of launch, testing them in the virtual simulation. I also worked to finalize the documentation.

Project Escape

Objective: The objective was to design a real-life application using 3 user inputs and 1 output with conditional logic (AND and OR gates).


For this project, I had to build and simulate a circuit that will have a real-life application to it. This design specification was an open-ended question, needing us to decide what our project needed to be on our own. For this project, I created an escape room that allows users to play the games at home even with the COVID-19 virus. For this project, I decided to make the game interesting by using a 3-bit counter and a AND and OR gate. This allows my project to have both conditional and sequential logic. For the project, I made the game challenging by having a 3 bit counter continuously run. With a switch, the user can toggle the 3-bit counter to run or stop. The game creates a challenge that allows the user to try to stop the correct 3-bit counter at the correct combination of LEDs. Then the user needs to get the correct combination of switches and if both the correct combination of switch and the correct combination of LEDs, the user won the game. For this project, I had to design the circuit online using a simulation software called multisim, and build the circuit using a protoboard system linked to software called MyDAQ. While building the breadboard, I used the built-in clock voltages with a frequency of around 1 Hertz allowing the user to have enough time for trying to solve the correct combination of switches. One challenge with building the breadboard was the lack of resources. With the shelter in place, having enough resources for building the mechanism was difficult. I fixed this issue by preplanning how to build the circuit on the protoboard. With this, I learned planning ahead and also wire resourcing. With these concepts, I was able to work on my other projects/activities without having these issues in mind. I also worked on creating a documentation to help show the design process for this project.

Work Experience is what distinguishes a great engineer from a good one. In the engineering academy, we are provided with numerous ways to grow and learn through experience. Apart from the enriching projects I do, I am able to meet with several industry professionals and learn from their experiences. I further my work experience through guest speakers, field trips/job shadows, and the mentorship program.

One engineering field I am interested in is Civil Engineering. Emma Schoental, a civil engineer, came to our school as a guest speaker on this topic. She explained the multiple types of civil engineers, as well as the types of projects that they work on. I was inspired by the process for a standard project. I learned how there were numerous engineering principles required for a Civil Engineer and there wasn’t just one way to tackle a problem. Another guest speaker was Maynard Holiday, a member of the team involved in the containment of the Chernobyl Powerplant. He discussed the methods of using robotics to help diagnose the powerplant as well as the methods used to contain the radiation from the explosion. As a member of the Dublin High VEX Robotics team, one of my ambitions is to use my knowledge of robotics to help benefit the world. As someone who did it, Mr. Holiday helped me understand how they used robotics to accomplish something that a human could not do. It was exciting to learn that one of my favorite passions, robotics, aligns with what people work on in the real world to help humanity.

One major field trip that I went to was the JA Job Shadow for Splunk. In this field trip, I was taught Splunk’s principles as well as their work in data sciences. The presenters discussed how the data they gathered propelled Splunk to be a major backend company for many other large corporations. The presenters that I met were an intern working in project management, and two data scientists. These presenters taught me how data is everywhere. Apart from the technical lessons, this field trip showed me how Splunk was able to have a fundamental work-life balance for their employees. It was great knowing that they can help the community while also ensuring their employees are treated properly. Another reason this field trip was interesting was that I was able to speak with the intern presenter. Being a PM and leading the sprint and storyboard has always been something interesting for me. I always felt like the PM was the leader for the people participating in the stories, who are in truth the brains for a company. Having the opportunity to talk to a PM intern allowed me to see what happens in the background. Another field trip that I went to was the Lawrence Livermore National Laboratory’s National Ignition Facility (NIF). There, I was able to see the process of how they conducted experiments in the laboratory. They explained that they initially run many simulations before starting up the laser. This interested me because of my enthusiasm for computer science. By using the laws of fusion and light equations, the group can predict the output and how long a reaction will occur during the test. These simulations are very effective since they validate the safety of their employees and receive an estimate for what to predict. This field trip showed me that computer science is not limited to just web development and data management, but the possibilities are huge such as simulations like what NIF does.

As part of the mentorship program, I can get an even closer look at my desired profession by meeting with industry workers, in either a one-on-one or group setting, to hear what insight they have and listen to their guidance. My wonderful mentor, Srinath Sura, is a manager at Facebook who has a lot of advice on how to pursue a career in computer science. One piece of advice that really stuck with me was when Mr. Sura discussed his definition of success. He believes that if someone is able to go to work and come back with a smile, they truly are successful. Along with getting advice from my mentor, the mentorship program also has professional building focuses where my mentor sits with me to work on my résumé and online portfolio. This has helped me a lot since I used to have a hard time putting myself on a piece of paper but with the help of Mr. Sura and other mentors, I can express myself better.
